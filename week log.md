# Socially intelligent robots: dimensions of human–robot interaction
 This reading felt like of a breath of fresh air compared to previous ones. It's not a traditional research study and it takes a more philosophical and historical approach to AI, which I really like coming from a cognitive science background.

 Why is social behavior and social context so hard for a robot to grasp?  I think a lot of it has to do with how we currently model the environment, which is not similar to how humans natively process information. Base modalities of input into the model are typically text and image not audio and video. These simpler representations are derived from the raw data but there is no higher-order sort of social processing that happens in those early cleaning stages.  for the strides we have needed to make to get to where we are in modern robotics, this method has made modeling a lot easier and we've generally concluded that it comes at almost no cost. But there is a cost: the loss of social cues and social behaviors.  From that in order to achieve this HRI objective, I would argue that change needs to come from these earliest layers where humans would initially incorporate these features to derive true underlying meaning. This is similar to why it's well known that AI struggles with complex emotions like comedy, sarcasm, or irony.

One thing I'm curious about is this claim the paper makes that robots need social skills when interacting with other robots. I would like to see what the robot-robot interaction (RRI) paradigm might look like and how it would differ from humans to robots. Although personally I can't at present think of an example where this use case is currently relevant (edit: potentially self-driving e.g. Waymo's driving near each other on the street).

 I think this reading has the most relevance to maybe folks that are interested in product or consumer-facing tools. I think this topic is less about task success and more about appeal and trust. It also plays a larger role in the sociological discussion of the role of AI in society, whether it should have a social component that makes it personable to humans. This article was published in 2007, before we even had real more humanoid robots out there and before we were able to get things like reinforcement learning to where they are today. It's interesting to see how this is still an open problem whereas a lot of other things are primarily solved. It speaks to the difficulty and complexity of social modeling.

# Expressing thought: improving robot readability with animation principles

 This reading confirms one of the observations I made in the previous one, where it seems like the benefit of a socially competent robot comes more from the need for trust and perception of the robot rather than actually influencing task outcomes. I think that's interesting because I would assume that a socially competent robot could actually influence performance but that is not the main draw.

 The main takeaway of this paper is just that humans need feedback too. We talk a lot in this class about what humans need to see or be able to do when they interact with a human. What the information passed back and forth should be and at what frequency and when, etc.  but what this paper highlights is that not only do humans desire to provide feedback to the model to improve itself but it also needs reaction from the model to feel more like an interactive system or like a transparent product.

 This paper was published in 2011 and given that both papers from this reading are quite old, I wonder how these methods do transfer to today's problems. The animation and experiment setup is quite rudimentary. We can get a lot more high-fidelity feedback from modern physics-based simulations. The big insights here are qualitative not quantitative, in my opinion with most value coming from the discussion and context portions, where they discuss the merits of such animations and feedback.
